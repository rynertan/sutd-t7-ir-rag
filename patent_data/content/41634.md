# Coffee aromatisation process.

## Abstract
Volatile aromatics entrained with gases during the wet ting and extraction of roasted and ground coffee beans, and partly contained within the extract itself, are collected by sep aration of these gases from the extract and by passing the gases through at least two condensers wherein the first con denser removes water and the last condenser 0 C to 5 C condenses the volatile aromatics. The collected volatile aromatics are combined with processed coffee extracts and or soluble coffee.

## Description
Coffee aromatisation process This invention is concerned with a process for collecting volatile aromatics liberated from roasted and ground coffee using gases which are present in the ground roast coffee and which act as a stripping medium and or carrier for the volatile aromatics, and employing the collected volatile aromatics to make an instant coffee product having improved organoleptic properties. The desirability of preparing an instant coffee product containing much of the aromatic materials originally contained within fresh roasted coffee beans, to provide an instant coffee having aroma and flavour comparable to fresh roasted coffee, has long been recognized. According to one published source Clifford, M.N., The Composition of Green and Roasted Coffee Beans , ProcessBiochemistry, pp. 13 19, May 1975 , taking fresh roasted coffee beans as containing 100 volatiles, during grinding of the roasted beans preliminary to the preparation of instant coffee a loss of about 14 of the volatiles is incurred. Further according to that source, during subsequent processing, i.e., extraction, concentration and drying, where no steps are taken to prevent the loss of volatiles, an additional 72 of the volatiles are lost. Thus, the final instant coffee retains a mere 14 of the original roast coffee volatiles. Various expedients have been suggested in the prior art in an attempt to prevent the loss of volatile aromatics.Thus, techniques have been developed which, for example, are directed to recovering the volatiles evolved during the grinding of the roasted coffee beans. Other techniques have been proposed for preventing the loss of volatiles during subsequent processing steps. For example, aromatic constituents contained within the coffee extract may be removed and collected prior to concentration of the extract, e.g., stripping of the extract, and the removed volatiles may then be returned to the extract after it has been concentrated. Various proposals have also been made to recover volatile aromatics which are normally lost during the extraction step, with which this invention is principally concerned. One such proposal, as disclosed in U.S. PatentNo. 2,432,759, for example, involves steam stripping volatile aromatics from a fresh extraction cell of roasted and ground coffee which has not been contacted with the extraction medium. After passing the steam through the extraction cell, the volatiles laden steam is then passed through a number of condensers. Condensed volatile aromatics may then be added back to the concentrated extract. That and similar techniques may present a number of disadvantages. The extra time and expense required may be economically undesirable. Additionally, steam stripping of unextracted ground roast coffee may subject the ground coffee to high temperatures which generally cause undesirable thermal degradation of its components. In another technique involving recovery of volatile aromatics during the extraction step, described in Canadian Patent No. 1,001,476 Bolt , liquid coffee extract is first produced by a battery of percolators. The conditions in the Bolt process are such that steam is internally generated within the coffee extract as it leaves the final percolator. The extract at this point is at a temperature in the range of from about 1000C to 127 0C. The liquid extract is then passed through a separation chamber where flash evaporation of water and volatile aromatics takes place and where the aromatics laden gases are separated from the liquid coffee extract. The volatile aromatics are then condensed and may be returned to the coffee extract either before or after that extract has been concentrated. Although steam is internally generated within the coffee extract in the Bolt technique, in contrast to the technique described above wherein an external source of steam is provided for stripping, the Bolt process may nevertheless still present a number of the disadvantages discussed above. Thus, as in conventional steam stripping techniques, the employment of high temperatures for the internal generation of steam within the coffee extract may also cause some undesirable thermal degradation of its components. vet another technique involving the recovery of volatile aromatics during the extraction or percolation step these terms conventionally being used interchangeably in the art involves collecting volatile aromatics entrained with the gases that are evolved during the wetting of fresh roasted and ground coffee with an extraction medium. In particular, as a result of roasting green coffee beans, carbon dioxide is produced, a substantial amount of which is occluded within the cells of the coffee even after the coffee beans have been ground. Additionally, air also fills the interstitial voids between the ground particles of coffee. The first step in extraction is to wet the dry, fresh roasted and ground coffee with a hot extraction medium.As the extraction medium enters a percolator normally at the bottom, in which the ground coffee is contained and begins fill the percolator, it gradually wets the roasted and ground coffee. While doing so, the extraction medium simultaneously displaces, with a piston like effect, carbon dioxide, air and volatile aromatics which become entrained with these gases. For the sake of convenience and clarity, the term aromatics laden gases will be used throughout this specification to designate the mixture of carbon dioxide, air and any volatile aromatics that may be entrained with these gases. During the wetting step, it is generally common to simply vent the aromatics laden gases to the atmosphere as they are being evolved to prevent the possibility of undesirable foaming in the coffee extract. At pages 297 300 of Volume 1 of Coffee ProcessingTechnology by Sivetz et al. AVI, 1963 , however, it is taught that there is a substantial amount of volatile aromatics entrained with the gases evolved during the wetting step of the percolator and that it is possible to recover these volatile aromatics by condensation. Sivetz et al. disclose taking the aromatics laden gases evolved by the wetting step and passing them through a liquid gas cyclone separator in which any extract that may have been carried over with the aromatics laden gases from the extraction system is separated. The separated extract is then combined with unprocessed extract produced during subsequent extraction of the ground roast coffee. The separated aromatics laden gases are then passed through an ice water 0 chiller maintained at a temperature of 0 C with iced cooling water wherein most of the water, acetic acid and the least volatile aromatic components are condensed and then separated from the remaining aromatics laden gases by subsequent passage through another liquid gas cyclone separator. Sivetz et al. teach that this first condensate is also to be combi ned with unprocessed coffee extract.Finally, the balance of the aromatics laden gases is then passed through a cold 0 trap which is cooled by Freon ll and dry ice to 73.33 C so that the aromatics laden gases are cooled to 45.560C.This second condensate, according to Sivetz et al., is also combined with unprocessed coffee extract. The Sivetz technique also has a number of disadvantages. Sivetz only treats the aromatics laden gases that are evolved during the wetting phase of the extraction step and therefore only obtains volatile aromatics that are entrained with this portion of aromatics laden gases. As will be discussed more fully hereinafter, applicant has discovered that additional aromatics laden gases are present within the coffee extract itself which contains even more desirable fractions of volatile aromatics than those contained with the aromatics laden gases evolved during the wetting phase of the extraction step. Sivetz has no appreciation or recognition of this. By treating only the aromatics laden gases evolved during the wetting phase, Sivetz fails to collect the additional and more desirable volatile aromatics which are still contained within the coffee extract. Moreover, by combining the first condensate with the unprocessed extract, Sivetz is uneconomically adding water back to the extract which subsequently will only have to be removed. Additionally, by combining the entire first condensate with the extract, Sivetz may also be adding the least volatile components to the extract among which are those which may have undesirable, harsh notes. Also contained within this first condensate are aromatic fractions which are relatively highly volatile. By Sivetz prescribed teachings, these fractions too are combined with unprocessed coffee extract where they will most probably be lost during subsequent processing. Finally, Sivetz requirement that the second condensate be obtained by employing a cold trap maintained at 0 a temperature of 73.33 C is frequently impractical on an industrial scale. The refrigeration equipment involved is generally uneconomical and difficult to run due two ice formation. This second condensate may also contain undesirable aromatic fractions, particularly a 34.440C fraction which has a distinct but objectionable sulphury note. Nevertheless, however, Sivetz teaches that the entire second condensate, 0 which may also contain the undesirable 34.44 C fraction as well, is to be combined with the unprocessed extract. Applicants have found a process for recovering volatile aromatics during the extraction of roasted and ground coffee that employs these recovered volatile aromatics to obtain an instant coffee product having improved in cup flavour and in jar aroma which avoids substantially all of the disadvantages associated with the prior art processes discussed above. The process of the present invention economically and desirably utilizes the gases which are present in fresh roasted and ground coffee to act as a stripping medium and carrier for volatile aromatics contained within the ground coffee. In particular, applicants treat and separate volatile aromatics entrained with the aromatics laden gases evolved during the wetting phase of the extraction step and additionally treat the entire coffee extract to separate even more desirable volatile aromatics contained therein.Applicants have also found that in addition to treating the aromatics laden gases evolved by the wetting step, it is also highly desirable to treat the entire extract so as to remove additional aromatics laden gases contained within the extract to recover very desirable volatile aromatics from these additionally removed aromatics laden gases which provide a more balanced and pleasing instant coffee product. By eliminating steam stripping of the dry roasted ground coffee, the extraction step is considerably shortened and simplified since there is no longer any disruption of the normal extraction operation. Despite the fact that the present invention does not resort to steam stripping where it might be expected that more volatile aromatics are obtained than with the technique of the present invention, quite unexpectedly, applicants have found that the total quantity of volatile aromatics obtained by the process of the present invention is substantially equal to that obtained by steam stripping.Thus, the present invention produces an instant coffee product having an intense and natural coffee like in jar aroma and a smooth, balanced in cup coffee flavour on reconstitution with water. The process of the present invention also avoids substantially all of the disadvantages associated with theSivetz technique discussed above. Whereas Sivetz treats only the aromatics laden gases evolved during the wetting step, the present invention treats these aromatics laden gases as well as the entire coffee extract to obtain additional and even more desirable volatile aromatics. In a preferred embodiment of the process of the present invention, fresh, roasted and ground coffee is wetted by an extraction medium while at the same time, unextracted coffee that has been previously wetted is simultaneously extracted. The aromatics laden gases that are evolved during the wetting step of the fresh, ground roasted coffee and the extract resulting from the extraction of the pre wetted coffee are concurrently passed through a separation chamber and then processed as described above. The entire process of the present invention is made more efficient and economical in this manner by simultaneously providing substantially uniform and constant rates of flow of both the aromatics laden gases and the extract from the separation chamber to the subsequent processing steps.Among other things, this further enables the aromatics laden gases to act as an even better stripping and carrier medium for the desirable volatile aromatics. Referring now to the drawings, and particularly toFigure 1, the process of the present invention is carried out as follows An extraction medium is provided to first enter line 10 into a percolator 12 containing fresh roasted and ground coffee. The extraction medium employed may simply be hot water or water containing coffee solubles. Preferably, the extraction medium is coffee extract prepared from a conventional battery of percolator cells not shown which are serially connected and through which hot water is passed countercurrently to provide the coffee extract. The preparation of such a coffee extract is well known to one skilled in the art. The extraction medium is pumped by a pump not shown into the bottom of percolator 12. The pressure created by the pump is just enough to force the extraction medium through percolator 12 and into a separation chamber 16 to be discussed more fully hereinbelow. Generally, the pressure existing in the extraction system is no more than about 0.34 bar above atmospheric. As the extraction medium wets the dry roasted and ground coffee, gases laden with volatile aromatics are evolved and displaced upwardly as the extraction medium rises in and fills percolator 12. These aromatics laden gases along with any coffee extract that may be carried over by the aromatics laden gases and any extract that may have been held up in the lines from previous extractions are passed through line 14 into a separation chamber 16. Separation chamber 16 is generally a liquid gas cyclone separator. The particular separator employed in the present invention is not critical as long as it can effect tively separate the aromatics laden gases from the liquid extract. Any commercially available separator may be employed provided it meets this criterion. In separation chamber 16, aromatics laden gases are separated from liquid extract and pass into line 18.Correspondingly, separated extract passes into line 20. As the wetting phase of the extraction step nears completion, the extraction of coffee solubles from the ground roast coffee begins as coffee extract sometimes referred to as draw off extract begins to leave percolator 12 and enters line 14. The ratio of the total weight of draw off extract leaving percolator 12 to the weight of coffee contained in the percolator prior to extraction is conventionally referred to as the draw off ratio. Generally, the larger the draw off ratio, the more aromatics will be extracted from the roast, ground coffee contained in percolator 12. However, owing to the practical physical and economical limitations of the subsequent processing steps in which substantially all of the water is ultimately removed, and because flavour damage during such concentration and drying steps is ordinarily proportional to the weight of water removed, a draw off ratio of from about 2.0 to 4.0 1 is usually employed. The temperature of the incoming extraction medium is controlled so that the draw off extract is at a temperature which is below its boiling point. The temperature of the initial draw off extract is generally about 600C to 700C and gradually rises as extraction continues to a peak temperature. Since the extraction medium is generally aqueous and the pressure, as discussed above, is essentially atmospheric, the maximum temperature of the draw off extract is 0 such that it is less than 100 C. Preferably, the temperature is in the range of from about 800C to 98 C. By keeping the temperature of the extract below its boiling point, possible thermal degradation of the heat sensitive components contained within the ground coffee and the extract itself is avoided. Without any interruption in the flow of material through line 14, the draw off extract is allowed to continuously flow into separation chamber 16 until the entire extract has passed through the chamber so as to remove additional aromatics laden gases entrained within the extract which in turn contains desirable volatile aromatics. As an alternative embodiment, it is possible to employ supplementary means not shown in the drawings to assist the stripping action. In accordance with a preferred embodiment of the process of the present invention, the foregoing described process is modified by making provision for the simultaneous introduction of both the draw off extract and the aromatics laden gases into the separation chamber. Efficiency is thus improved by having substantially uniform and constant rates of flow throughout the entire cell cycle time of the separated extract and separated aromatics laden gases into the subsequent processing steps. Not only does this alternative embodiment make the process of the present invention more economically desirable but, as will be discussed, provides for an even greater effectiveness for recovering the desirable volatile aromatics. As was discussed earlier in connection with the embodiment of the present invention shown in Figure 1, it generally takes about one half of an extraction cycle or 50 of the cell cycle time for the wetting step of percolator 12 to be completed. During this time, except for extract that may have been held up in the lines from previous extractions, essentially only evolved aromatics laden gases from percolator 12 enter separation chamber 16 during the first half of the extraction cycle and into the subsequent aromatics laden gases treatment steps for condensing and recovering the volatile aromatics contained therein. During that same first half of the extraction cycle, however, the extract treatment means may not therefore be operating at its most efficient design capacity. This may particularly be true in, for example, aromatic removal means 26 where a steam stripping column is typically employed. Due to the relatively low flow rate of extract during the first half of the cell cycle, the liquidgas contact in the column may not be the most efficient for removing the aromatic constituents contained in the extract since the column would be designed for the maximum peak flow rate of extract expected to pass through it. Furthermore, when the aromatics laden gases are evolved during the wetting step, it is found that there is a surge of gases emitted from percolator 12 almost immediately and the flow rate of the gases rapidly tapers off to a relatively constant flow. This is demonstrated in Figure 3.The curve delineated by triangles A represents the rate of flow of aromatics laden gases leaving separation chamber 16 during an entire cell cycle when employing the embodiment of the present invention shown in Figure 1. As is seen, the flow rate of the gases peaks almost immediately to a flow rate of approximately 47.5 cubic meters per hour then tapers off to a flow rate of about 5.0 cubic meters per hour. The condensers 40, 42 and 44 for treating the aromatics laden gases, for example, must however be designed to be able to treat the gases at the peak rate of flow into this treatment system. Accordingly, when the aromatics laden gases are entering the condensers subsequent to the peak flow rate, the condensers may not be operating at their most maximum design efficiency. In this preferred embodiment therefore, applicant has found a means for providing a substantially uniform, constant flow rate for both the draw off extract as well as for the aromatics laden gases into the respective treatment zones for essentially the entire cell cycle time. The employment of a pre fill cell is disclosed in U.S. Patent No. 3,995,067. More particularly, referring to Figure 4 in which the same reference numerals are employed as in Figure 1 to indicate like elements, the process of this preferred embodiment is carried out by first providing an extraction medium to enter line 10 into a percolator 12. As in the embodiment shown in Figure 1, the extraction medium employed may simply be hot water, water containing coffee solubles or coffee extract obtained from a conventional battery of percolator cells not shown . Percolator 12, commonly referred to as the drawoff cell contains unextracted ground roast coffee which has already been pre wetted in a previous extraction cycle. Thus, substantially all of the aromatics laden gases that are evolved during such a wetting step of fresh, ground roast coffee have already been evolved and recovered during such previous extraction cycle. Although the coffee in percolator 12 has not as yet been extracted, it is nevertheless completely filled with extraction medium as a result of the prewetting step. Accordingly, as soon as extraction medium is pumped not shown into percolator 12 by line 10, draw off extract immediately begins to flow from the percolator at a constant and uniform rate and is split into lines 14 and 14A, respectively. The portion of the draw off extract entering line 14 is passed directly into separation chamber 16 in which aromatics laden gases entrained within the extract are removed. The separated extract and aromatics laden gases are then treated in a manner similar to that described earlier. The remaining portion of draw off extract entering line 14A is simultaneously introduced into percolator 8 which, in this preferred embodiment, acts as a pre fill cell in which fresh, ground roast coffee is subjected only to a wetting step in this extraction cycle. As draw off extract from line 14A enters percolator 8 and wets the dry roasted and ground coffee, gases laden with volatile aromatics are evolved and displaced as the draw off extract fills the percolator. These aromaticsladen gases along with any coffee extract that may be carried over by the aromatics laden gases are passed through line 14B into separation chamber 16 at the same time that draw off extract is also entering separation chamber 16 from line 14. The aromatics laden gases are separated from the liquid extract in chamber 16 and the separated gases and separated extract are then treated in a manner similar to that described earlier. When the entire extraction cycle is completed, the now pre wetted coffee in percolator 8 is ready to be extracted in a subsequent extraction cycle in a manner similar to that just described for the pre wetted coffee in percolator 12 and, correspondingly, new fresh ground roast coffee is provided in another percolator not shown to be pre wetted in the manner just described for percolator 8. The process is thus carried out on a continuous basis. In this preferred embodiment, the unextracted coffee contained in percolator 12 is extracted for an increased period of time, i.e., extraction occurs throughout the entire cell cycle. The efficiency of the extraction is thus increased by allowing more time for volatile aromatics and other coffee material to dissolve in the extraction medium. In addition, because the draw off extract from percolator 12 is split into two streams one of the streams, line 14A, being used to wet and fill pre fill percolator cell 8 , the amount of extraction medium entering percolator 12 may be increased thereby resulting in a more desirable higher draw off ratio. Accordingly, the draw off ratio based on the total weight of draw off extract leaving percolator 12 to the weight of coffee originally contained in the percolator may range from 4.0 to 6.5 1, most preferably from 5.0 to 6.0 1. Upon leaving percolator 12, the draw off extract may be split such that a draw off ratio of about 2.0 to 4.5 1, and preferably 3.0 1 is passed directly into the separation chamber with the remaining portion of the drawoff extract, 2.0 1, being passed into percolator 8. Similarly, in this preferred embodiment, the wetting of the fresh, ground roast coffee contained in percolator 8 also desirably takes place throughout the entire cell cycle. In this manner, the aromatics laden gases evolved during the wetting step leave percolator 8 at a correspondingly slower rate of flow. Additionally, by treating the draw off extract in separation chamber 16 to remove aromatics laden gases entrained therein at the same time that evolved aromatics laden gases from percolator 8 are introduced into separation chamber 16, the flow rate of the gases leaving chamber 16 is substantially uniform and constant.Returning to Figure 3, this is demonstrated in the curve delineated by squares a which represents the flow rate of aromatics laden gases leaving chamber 16 for a complete cell cycle when employing a pre fill cell. As is seen, the peak representing the initial surge of evolved gases is considerably flatter and tapers off more gradually to a rate of flow which is substantially higher and more uniform than that associated with the embodiment shown in the curve delineated with triangles A . This ability to provide a substantially uniform and constant rate of flow of the aromatics laden gases into the gases treatment zone increases the effectiveness of the present invention by enabling greater recovery of volatile aromatics. The condensers employed for treating the aromaticsladen gases, for example, may now be designed for a gas flow rate which is substantially uniform and constant rather than for a flow rate which has a surge peak thereby increasing the efficiency of these condensers. So too, the efficiency of the other equipment employed in the other processing steps is also correspondingly increased. Moreover, by providing a more uniform and constant flow rate of the aromatics laden gases, the ability of the gases to function as a stripping medium and carrier is also increased so that the aromatics laden gases more effectively strip the desirable volatile aromatics from the coffee in percolator 8, from the extract in separation chamber 16, and from the condensate in condenser 40 in which desirable low boiling fractions may have become entrained to ultimately recover these volatile aromatics in condensers 42 and 44 and be incorporated into the final instant coffee product. As alternative embodiments not shown in Figure 4 , percolator 8 acting as a pre fill cell may be pre wetted by sources other than draw off extract obtained from percolator 12. Thus, for example, if desired the extraction medium entering line 10 may be split to simultaneously enter both percolators 8 and 12, respectively, and the draw off extract leaving percolator 12 and aromatics laden gases leaving percolator 8 may be directly connected to separation chamber 16.In yet another alternative, a portion of separated extract leaving separation chamber 16 by line 20 or a portion of stripped extract leaving aromatic removal means 26 by line 28 may be split from these lines and employed as the medium for wetting the fresh, ground roast coffee in percolator 8 in which the output line, line 14B, is directly connected to separation chamber 16. Simultaneously, an external source of extraction medium is provided for extracting the pre wetted coffee contained in percolator 12 which is also directly connected to separation chamber 16 by line 14. Line 14A, in this alternative embodiment, would of course not be employed.In each of these alternative embodiments, the draw off ratios are adjusted so that the amount of extract entering concentration means 30 is in each instance in the range of about 2.0 4.5 1 and preferably about 3.0 1 and the amount of extract entering percolator 8 is about 2.0 1.
